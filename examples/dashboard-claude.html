Demand Estimation: How Price Affects Volume

Log-log regression: ln(Volume) = β · ln(Price) + controls. The coefficient on ln(Price) is the price elasticity of demand.

Key insight: Controls matter enormously. The naive model estimates an elasticity of −4.59, suggesting a 1% price increase cuts volume by 4.6%. But this is omitted variable bias: organic avocados are both pricier and sold in far smaller volumes. Once we control for type, the elasticity drops to −1.05. Adding market and year fixed effects barely changes it (−1.04), confirming the type distinction was the key confounder.

Interpretation: Avocado demand is approximately unit elastic — a 1% price increase reduces quantity demanded by ~1%. This is consistent with avocados being a popular but substitutable grocery item. The organic indicator shows that organic markets sell exp(−3.13) ≈ 96% fewer units than conventional, even at the same price.

Additional Findings
Seasonality: Prices peak in fall (Sep–Nov) when supply tightens between the Mexican and California harvest seasons. Volume peaks in late winter/spring (Feb–Mar) driven by Super Bowl and Cinco de Mayo demand.
Organic growth: Organic market share nearly doubled from 2.0% in 2015 to 3.9% in 2020, even as total market volume grew 35%. Organic prices trended down from $1.67 to $1.51, suggesting supply is catching up with demand.
Market concentration: Los Angeles alone accounts for more volume than the next two markets combined (New York + Dallas). The West Coast and Texas dominate, reflecting proximity to Mexican import routes and strong Hispanic food culture.
2017 price spike: Average conventional prices jumped from $1.10 to $1.29 in 2017 due to a poor California harvest and reduced Mexican imports. Despite higher prices, volume continued to grow — suggesting a demand shift that partially offsets price sensitivity.
